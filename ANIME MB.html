Chapter One: The Boy Beneath the Willow

A Second Life

Freht remembered dying. Not the details. Those had faded into fragments long before he could even speak. A flash of light. A feeling of falling. Voices. Then silence. Yet from the moment his eyes first opened in the world of Tallus, he knew one thing with absolute certainty: This was not his first life.

The realization came not as a revelation but as an uncomfortable fact, like waking from a dream only to find yourself trapped inside another. His newborn body was weak, helpless, and unable to obey the command of a mind that remembered adulthood. He could think and reason. He could remember pieces of another existence. But he could not even hold up his own head. The first face he ever saw was his mother's. Soft brown hair framed a weathered but beautiful face touched by years beneath the sun. Her hands were rough from labor yet gentle whenever they held him.

"Look at him, Samuel," she whispered one evening. "He has your eyes."

A large man stepped into view. Samuel carried himself like a wolf that had chosen to live among sheep. His dark skin was scarred from countless battles. One jagged mark crossed his left cheek. His frame seemed impossibly large to infant Freht. Yet whenever he held him, Samuel's hands moved with surprising care. The giant mercenary became something else entirely when holding his son.

"He has your stubborn stare," Samuel answered.

The couple laughed. Freht listened and learned.

The World of Tallus

Their home stood in the countryside several miles from the nearest village. A sturdy two-story house built from gray stone bricks. Its roof was made of cedar shingles darkened by years of rain. A wooden fence enclosed a generous yard where goats wandered lazily among patches of grass. Several cows grazed in nearby fields. Apple trees lined the southern side of the property. A vegetable garden occupied the eastern side. Behind the house stretched open countryside covered in rolling hills and scattered forests.

For Samuel and Yara, it was paradise. For Freht, it became the center of his new universe. His first year passed slowly. Painfully slow. His mind raced far faster than his body. He could understand more than anyone realized.

He listened to conversations, observed routines, and collected information. At night he lay awake staring at wooden ceiling beams while piecing together the rules of this new world. Tallus. That was its name; he heard it often. Merchants spoke of distant kingdoms. Travelers discussed border disputes. Priests passing through the region spoke of cities far beyond the horizon. The world was vast. Far larger than the tiny room where he slept.

By age two he had learned the language. At least mentally. Speaking remained difficult; his tongue refused to cooperate. Simple words emerged as mangled sounds. The experience was humiliating. Inside his mind, he formed complete sentences. Outside, he sounded like every other toddler. Yara simply smiled whenever he grew frustrated.

"Patience, little one."

She often said those words. Patience. A lesson Freht would hear many times. His mother became the center of his early education. Yara traveled regularly to nearby villages. She treated fevers. Delivered babies. Bandaged wounds. Prepared medicines from herbs gathered in the countryside. Whenever possible, she brought Freht with her.

By age three he rode comfortably in a wooden cart beside jars of medicinal plants. He watched carefully. A surprising number of people arrived at their door seeking help. Farmers, travelers, old widows, orphaned children. Yara treated all of them equally. Though many left eggs, vegetables, bread, or small coins in gratitude.

Each evening, she prayed. Not elaborate rituals, not grand ceremonies. Simple prayers. Prayers to a god that he didn’t know. Prayers of gratitude. Prayers for those suffering. Prayers for strength. Freht often listened from the doorway. There was a quiet certainty in her faith that fascinated him.

Quiet Lessons

Samuel taught different lessons. Not with words, but with actions. The retired mercenary woke before sunrise every morning without fail. He repaired fences, split firewood, tended livestock, maintained tools, and worked fields. His strength seemed endless. Yet what impressed Freht most was discipline. The Black Lion of the Border Wars—whose name still carried weight among travelers—lived no differently than any common farmer.

No boasting. No stories. No displays of glory. Only work, steady and relentless. When visitors recognized him, Samuel usually changed the subject. The wars belonged to another life. This one belonged to his family.

At age four, Freht began reading. Officially, Yara was teaching him how to read. Unofficially, he absorbed knowledge far faster than either parent expected. Within months he could read simple passages. By five he was reading religious texts (which he wasn't incredibly interested in), trade ledgers, and any written material he could find.

Samuel suspected something unusual. One evening Freht overheard his parents talking.

"He's too quick," Samuel said.

Yara smiled.

"God grants different gifts to different people."

"I've known scholars slower than our son."

"Then perhaps he will become one," Yara chuckled. "Or perhaps he's inherited your stubbornness and my bad habits."

Freht smiled secretly from upstairs, hearing his parents compliment him. Yet despite his advantages, childhood remained childhood. He still climbed trees, still chased goats, still fell into mud, still scraped knees, and still laughed. The memories of his former life grew more distant with each passing year. Not forgotten. But softened.

Tallus felt surreal.

The Sixth Birthday

Then came his sixth birthday, and that morning began differently. Samuel knocked on his bedroom door before sunrise.

"Get dressed.”

Freht sat upright. Immediately alert. His father's voice carried more seriousness than usual.

Outside, dawn painted the hills gold. Mist drifted across the fields. The world felt quiet. Waiting.

Samuel stood near the old willow tree at the edge of their property. Something rested against the trunk. A wooden sword.

Freht's heart quickened. Samuel picked it up and tossed it toward him. The practice blade felt surprisingly heavy. The retired mercenary folded his arms. For a moment neither spoke. Then Samuel smiled. Not the smile of a farmer. Not the smile of a father. The smile of a warrior.

"The world beyond these fields is not peaceful, son."

His voice carried the weight of experience.

"I pray you'll never need these lessons."

He pointed toward the horizon.

"But if that day comes, I refuse to let you face it unprepared."

Freht tightened his grip on the wooden sword. A strange excitement stirred inside him. Part fear, part anticipation. Samuel stepped forward. The morning sun illuminated countless scars across his father's arms.

"First lesson."

The Black Lion lowered into a fighting stance.

"Everyone thinks a sword is for killing."

His gaze sharpened.

"They're wrong."

The old warrior raised his practice blade.

"A sword is for protecting what matters."

Then he attacked. And Freht's true life in Tallus began. Training was intense.

Chapter Two: Foundations of Strength

Before Dawn

The day began in darkness, every day without exception. A heavy hand would knock against the wooden door of Freht's room.

"Up."

Samuel's deep voice carried through the house.

The first few weeks were miserable. Freht often woke with aching muscles and sore joints. His previous life memories had fooled him into believing he possessed maturity. His six-year-old body quickly corrected that misconception. His father showed no mercy. Not because he was cruel, but because the world would be far less forgiving. The morning air was always cold. Dew covered the grass, stars still hung overhead. The horizon only hinted at the coming sunrise. Samuel would already be waiting beneath the old willow tree. Practice sword in hand.

Arms folded. Watching. Always watching.

"Late."

That was usually the first thing he said, even when Freht arrived early. Especially when Freht arrived early. The lesson was simple: “A warrior's greatest enemy is complacency.”

Training always began with running. No weapons. No combat. No glory. Just movement. Father and son ran across dirt roads winding through the countryside. Over hills. Through meadows past grazing cattle. Into nearby forests. Samuel maintained a steady pace. Never fast enough to leave Freht behind, never slow enough for him to relax.

At first, Freht hated it. His lungs burned, his legs screamed, his feet ached. Every morning he wondered why anyone would willingly do such a thing. Samuel finally answered after weeks of complaints:

"A sword is useless if you're too tired to lift it."

That ended the discussion.

Strength Before Skill

After running came labor, real labor. Not exercises, work: carrying water buckets, splitting small logs, moving stones, repairing fences, cleaning animal stalls, and feeding livestock.

Samuel claimed farmers and warriors required the same thing: “Strength earned honestly.” Not strength gained through vanity. By the end of every session, Freht's arms trembled from exhaustion. Yet slowly he noticed changes. The buckets became lighter. The hills were less steep. The chores were less overwhelming as his body adapted.

Only after months of preparation did Samuel begin true martial training. The practice sword was carved from oak. Simple, heavy, unforgiving.

Freht had imagined heroic duels. Swift strikes, magnificent techniques. Instead, Samuel spent weeks correcting how he stood, how he breathed, how he held the weapon.

"Again."

Freht adjusted his stance.

"No. Again."

He corrected it.

"No."

Again. And again. And again.

The repetition seemed endless. One morning Freht finally snapped.

"It's just standing!"

Samuel immediately struck him across the shoulder with the practice sword. The impact stung. Freht yelped. His father nodded.

"Your stance was wrong."

The lesson needed no further explanation. Occasionally Samuel shared fragments of wisdom from his years as a mercenary. Never stories glorifying battle, never tales of conquest, only lessons.

  • "Most fights are decided before swords leave their sheaths."
  • "Fear makes people stupid."
  • "The dead warrior and the proud warrior are often the same man."
  • "Never underestimate a desperate opponent."
  • "The worst fight is the one you fight in your mind. A calm mind wins wars."

These lessons fascinated Freht more than swordsmanship itself. They carried the weight of experience. The wisdom of a man who had survived where countless others had fallen.

Training ended when the sun reached its highest point. By then Freht was usually drenched in sweat, bruised, exhausted, and hungry enough to eat an entire goat. The smell of lunch often drifted from the house before he reached it. Fresh bread, vegetable stew, roasted meat. His mother's cooking became the reward awaiting every brutal morning. A warrior ate, he did not complain, he did not waste.

Afternoons belonged to Yara.

The atmosphere changed immediately. Gone were the demands of physical endurance. Gone was Samuel's stern discipline. Yara's lessons required patience instead. Sometimes even more patience. The dining table became a classroom; books covered its surface. Handwritten scrolls, religious texts, maps, medical journals. Pages filled with strange symbols.

Freht learned reading first. Then writing. Then arithmetic. His previous life memories helped immensely. Within a year he read more fluently than many adults in nearby villages. Yet Yara never allowed arrogance.

"Knowledge without wisdom creates fools."

Another lesson Freht heard often. One autumn afternoon Yara introduced him to magic. Not with grand demonstrations. Not with explosions. Simply by placing a small crystal upon the table.

"Everything created by the gods contains mana. This is a small mana crystal."

Freht leaned forward; the crystal glowed faintly. Like moonlight trapped within glass.

"Living things contain more."

She placed a hand over her heart.

"We are vessels."

For months Freht learned nothing but meditation. Much to his disappointment, he expected spells or grimoires that could aid him in learning spells. Instead, he sat quietly. Breathing, listening, feeling, and searching. At first, there was nothing. Then one day he noticed it. A faint warmth, a current flowing beneath his skin, gentle. Alive.

Mana. The moment lasted only seconds. Yet Yara smiled.

"You found it."

Freht grinned. His journey into magic had begun.

Healing magic became his first discipline. Yara considered it the most important. The simplest spell allowed him to soothe pain from small cuts and bruises. The process required concentration. Mana flowed from his body into another. Too much could exhaust the caster. Too little accomplished nothing.

The first time Freht successfully eased a wound on his own arm, he stared in amazement. The cut slowly closed. Leaving only faint pink skin. It felt miraculous.

Yara shook her head. "No." She smiled. "It is a gift."

The second discipline involved protection. Ward magic. Unlike healing, wards required structure, precision, and discipline. Freht learned to shape mana into temporary barriers. Yara tried to explain to him how to visualize simple circles of light: tiny shields capable of blocking rain or weak impacts. But to Freht’s mind, that didn’t make sense. Instead, he imagined a semi-transparent, shimmering barrier that floated in front of him, often with a soft blue glowing magical aura.

“That’s an odd shape,” Yara looked surprised.

His first somewhat successful ward lasted only a few seconds. Samuel immediately threw an apple at him. The apple bounced harmlessly away. Freht nearly fell over from surprise.

His father laughed. A rare sound.

"You'll need stronger ones."

The next day, it was the same routine: getting up early, running, and working around the house until midday. But today felt different. Elemental magic proved far more difficult. Especially wind. Unlike healing, wind demanded control over external forces. Freht spent countless afternoons trying to move feathers across the table. Sometimes they twitched. Sometimes nothing happened. Sometimes he accidentally launched them into Yara's face. Which she found endlessly amusing. Eventually, he produced his first genuine gust. Strong enough to scatter papers across the room. The success felt glorious. The cleanup less so.

Lightning remained the most dangerous, and therefore, the most restricted. Yara approached it cautiously.

"The power to harm should always come after the power to heal."

Freht never forgot those words. His first lessons involved little more than generating tiny sparks between his fingertips. Tiny blue arcs danced across his skin. Beautiful, mesmerizing, terrifying. Even Samuel seemed wary whenever lightning was involved. Freht could barely create more than a flicker. But he sensed its potential. Like a sleeping dragon waiting to awaken.

The next day at noon, Yara explained in more detail the magic that was used around the continent. She whipped out a big old book that had a weird circle.

“I taught you how to use magic, but I haven’t taught you what magic really is,”

Yara exclaimed while flipping over the pages.

According to some very old wizards around the age of mithril, they describe magic as an extraordinary energy that dwells within the body of every living being, and in the world flowing like a river, sometimes forming deposits by solidifying on the earth. It is also simultaneously an extra-dimensional energy. For now, that’s all you must study, remember to practice the spells that I taught you. In the next couple of days, we’ll practice the other elements: fire, water, and earth.

Freht followed through with his mother's teachings. He knew it was going to be complicated, even if he had memories from a more advanced life. By the time Freht had gained a basic understanding of healing, wards, wind, and lightning, he believed he was finally beginning to understand magic. His mother disagreed. One evening after supper, Yara sat beside the chimney while mending a torn sleeve. The firelight danced across her face.

"You are learning spells," she said.
"Isn't that the same thing?"
"No."

The answer came immediately.

"Knowing how to swing a sword does not make you a warrior."

A familiar lesson from Samuel. Yara smiled. "Magic is no different."

The Deities of Tallus

During this alone time, Freht thought to himself: if he was here in this world and was reborn, then where was Andrew? Was he also reborn in this world? If so, how was he doing? There was also no Jesus Christ in this world, no one true God. So, another question arose in his head. If there was no Christ in this world, did that mean there was no concept of original sin? Did that mean humanity from this world was never redeemed? If so, who are the deities of this world?

The next morning began very differently. Instead of training, Yara took a moment to explain the different views of the world. The morning fire crackled softly as Freht sat beside his mother. Yara set aside her book and smiled.

"There are many faiths in Tallus, understanding them is key..."

The Church of the Guiding Light

"The largest faith worships the Veil Goddess. They believe she protected humanity long ago and that order and justice are sacred."

The Great Five Gods

"Some people worship many gods instead of one. They pray to a god of war before battle, a god of harvest before planting crops, or a god of wisdom before making important decisions."

Calabath and the Forest Faiths

"The elves, druids, and some tribes honor nature. They believe spirits live within forests, rivers, and mountains."

The Dragon Cults

"Others believe dragons are divine beings."
"Real dragons?" Freht's eyes widened.
"Very real dragons... Because being powerful does not automatically make one worthy of worship."

The Fallen Ones & The Cults of Darkness

"There are people who believe ancient gods once protected the world and will someday return."

Her expression grew serious.

"There are also dark cults. They seek power above all else and often harm innocent people to obtain it."

Freht sat quietly for a moment.

"Mother, which faith is true?"

Yara placed a hand upon his head.

"Many people will give you different answers, my son. But this is what I believe. I’m a servant of the Veil Goddess. Learn. Listen. Observe. Judge people by their actions, not merely by the symbols they wear. A man may carry a holy symbol and still be cruel. Another may follow a strange faith and yet show kindness."

She tapped his chest lightly.

"Wisdom begins here, Freht. Learn what people believe, but always seek the truth for yourself."

The boy nodded, committing every word to memory.

Chapter Three: Years of Preparation

The Routine of Growth

The years that followed were defined by routine, discipline, and steady growth. From the age of six onward, Freht's life settled into a pattern that rarely changed. Before the first light of dawn touched the hills surrounding their home, Samuel was already awake, waiting in the training yard.

The old mercenary believed strength was built through repetition rather than talent.

Every morning began the same. Running. Endless running through dirt roads, forest trails, and rocky hills. Afterward came exercises that left every muscle burning. Pushes, pulls, climbing, lifting stones, carrying buckets of water, chopping wood, and countless drills designed to strengthen the body.

Freht complained often during his younger years. Samuel ignored every complaint.

"The battlefield does not care how tired you are."

By the time Freht reached ten years old, the complaints disappeared. By twelve, he often completed the exercises without being told. By fifteen, he possessed the physique of someone far older than his age. Though still short at only five feet three inches tall, his frame was dense with muscle earned through years of labor. Broad shoulders, strong legs, calloused hands, and the endurance to work from sunrise until nightfall became normal for him.

Samuel often remarked that Freht lacked the size of a warrior. But he never lacked the strength.

The Mace and Shield

Combat training became increasingly difficult as the years passed. At first, Samuel taught him with wooden sticks. Then came practice swords. Then spears. Axes. Knives. Every weapon a soldier might encounter.

Freht learned the basics of each. How to strike. How to defend. How to move. How to survive. Yet over time it became clear that certain weapons suited him more than others.

Freht lacked the reach needed to excel with spears. He found swords effective but unremarkable. The weapon that truly felt natural in his hands was the mace. Unlike a sword, the mace cared little for cutting edges. A strong strike could break bones through armor. Crush shields. Shatter helmets.

Samuel approved immediately.

"A mace doesn't ask permission from steel."

As Freht entered his teenage years, training shifted toward the use of a steel mace and shield. The shield became his constant companion. Samuel drilled him relentlessly. Shield positioning. Deflecting blows. Closing distance. Counterattacking. Maintaining balance. For hours they would spar beneath the sun until both were soaked in sweat.

Bruises became common, broken skin became common, failure became common, and improvement came slowly. But it came.

The Mind and The Elements

The afternoons were less stressful. Samuel hardened the body, Yara cultivated the mind. Reading, writing, mathematics, and history. She taught patiently, encouraging questions Samuel would have dismissed with a grunt. Freht developed a genuine love for books. Knowledge fascinated him almost as much as magic.

Magic lessons became increasingly complex as the years passed. Like every living being in Tallus, Freht possessed mana. Like every student of magic, he struggled.

Fire magic became his greatest frustration. By fifteen, after nearly a decade of effort, the greatest fire spell he could produce was a small steady flame sufficient to ignite kindling or light a campfire. Nothing more. No fireballs. No walls of flame. No torrents of destruction. Just a modest flame. The limitation irritated him greatly. Yara constantly reminded him that every mage had strengths and weaknesses. Freht remained unconvinced.

Water magic proved easier. He learned to manipulate small quantities of water. The same applied to air magic. He could create gusts. Push objects. Enhance movement slightly. Control airflow around himself.

Earth magic was another matter entirely. Earth responded to him with surprising ease. Stone barriers. Raised walls. Earthen spikes. Reinforced foundations. Defensive structures. These came naturally. By fifteen, Yara considered his earth magic to be approaching an intermediate level.

The same could be said for ward magic. Protective barriers became one of his specialties. Simple shields. Defensive circles. He lacked the power of experienced mages, but his control was dependable.

Lightning magic fascinated him from the beginning. Perhaps because it demanded precision rather than raw power. Unlike fire, lightning responded. Slowly. Reluctantly. But it responded. By fifteen, he could produce controlled electrical discharges, stun targets, and direct bolts over short distances. Nothing battlefield-changing. Yet impressive for his age.

The Art of Healing

Healing magic became another area of competence. Under Yara's instruction, Freht learned how mana interacted with the body. How wounds closed. How flesh repaired itself. How exhaustion could be eased. How injuries could be stabilized.

True healing remained difficult. Life was complex. The human body even more so. Yet by fifteen, he could reliably treat cuts, bruises, sprains, and moderate injuries. Enough to aid travelers, farmers, and neighbors. Yara still brought him along when helping the sick. Those experiences taught lessons no textbook ever could.

Potion brewing soon followed. Yara insisted that not every problem required magic. Sometimes a healer needed herbs. Knowledge. Patience. Practical skill.

Freht learned to identify medicinal plants growing throughout the region. He learned which roots reduced fever. Which leaves stopped bleeding. Which flowers soothed pain. Which ingredients became poison when mixed improperly.

Brewing potions demanded precision. A mistake measured in seconds could ruin an entire batch.

At first, he struggled. Several mixtures exploded. Others solidified into useless sludge. One particularly terrible attempt filled the house with a smell so awful Samuel refused to enter for two days.

Over time his skills improved. By fifteen, Freht could reliably prepare basic healing tonics, restorative brews, antiseptic mixtures, and common medicinal remedies. Yara considered this knowledge every bit as valuable as combat magic. Freht eventually agreed.

The Messenger

Years passed quietly. Seasons changed. The farm prospered. Freht grew alongside it. Not into a prodigy. Not into a legendary hero. But into something perhaps more valuable. A capable young man.

Strong. Disciplined. Educated. Able to fight. Able to heal. Able to work. Able to endure. The lessons of Samuel and Yara had become part of him.

On an otherwise ordinary evening shortly after his fifteenth birthday, a rider arrived at the house. The horse was exhausted. The rider carried official markings unfamiliar to Freht.

A sealed letter was delivered. Addressed solely to Samuel.

Freht watched as his father broke the seal. Samuel read the contents in silence. The color slowly drained from his face. His jaw tightened. The relaxed expression he had worn for years disappeared.

For a long moment, he simply stared at the parchment. Then he folded it carefully. When he finally looked up, his eyes carried a seriousness Freht had never seen before. And in that instant, he knew something had changed. Something from Samuel's past had finally found its way back to their doorstep.
